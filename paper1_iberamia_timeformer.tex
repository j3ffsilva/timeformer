\begin{abstract}
Words change meaning over time, yet most computational models treat token
representations as epoch-independent. We study temporal traceability: the
property that the same surface token, queried at different epochs, occupies
semantically appropriate neighborhoods in representation space. A model may use
epoch information to improve predictive accuracy without exposing a traceable
\texttt{token@time} representation --- and this gap is the focus of the paper.

We address it with Timeformer, a family of Transformer-style encoders organized
as a controlled ablation chain. Four variants add temporal capacity in successive
steps: a static baseline, a globally time-conditioned encoder, a token-time
encoder that conditions each token embedding on the epoch before self-attention
is applied, and a memory-augmented extension that consults causal historical
prototypes. All variants are trained with masked language modeling on a
controlled synthetic benchmark in which the temporal trajectory of each target
item is known and evaluation labels are withheld from training.

The main finding is that explicit token-time interaction is the effective
mechanism for traceability. The Token-Time Transformer produces a neighborhood
drift of \(-0.614\) for drifting subjects across epochs, compared to \(-0.231\)
for the static baseline --- a difference stable across seeds and visible in
class-specific trajectory curves. Probe-based accuracy gains are modest and
seed-sensitive, confirming that geometric diagnostics are more appropriate than
label recoverability for evaluating traceability. The memory-augmented variant
does not produce a robust improvement over token-time interaction; the oracle
diagnostic identifies mean-prototype aggregation as the failure point,
motivating sense-aware memory mechanisms in future work.

\keywords{Temporal semantics \and Semantic change \and Transformer models \and Traceability \and Diagnostic evaluation}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% Target length: 1.0--1.25 pages.
Words do not keep a fixed meaning across time. A term that is associated with
one set of events, practices, or communities in an earlier period may later
become associated with another. For computational models, this raises a
traceability problem: when the same surface token appears in different epochs,
we need to inspect not only what the model predicts, but also whether its
representation places that token in the appropriate temporal semantic
neighborhood. Without a way to inspect temporal movement in the representation, downstream
analyses risk collapsing distinct historical states into a single timeless
representation.
This limits our ability to audit semantic movement, diagnose temporal
generalization, and explain why a model treats the same lexical item
differently across periods.

The dominant computational treatment of this problem has been lexical semantic
change detection (LSCD), often based on diachronic word embeddings or related
distributional representations trained over time-sliced corpora. This line of
work has produced important methods and benchmarks for measuring semantic
change across periods~\cite{hamilton2016diachronic,schlechtweg2020semeval,tahmasebi2021survey}.
However, many approaches compare representations across independently modeled
or post-hoc aligned periods. Even when temporal information is available to a
model, the resulting representation is not always exposed as a directly
queryable object of the form \texttt{token@time}. As a result, the temporal
behavior of a lexical item may be visible only indirectly through aggregate
scores, rather than through the geometry of the representation itself.
Furthermore, a model that receives epoch information to improve a prediction
objective does not necessarily produce a traceable representation: it may shift
its outputs while leaving the geometric neighborhood of the token largely
unchanged. Predictive performance and representational traceability are
separable properties.

This paper studies this separation directly. We say a representation is
\emph{temporally traceable} if the same surface token, queried at different
epochs, occupies neighborhoods that reflect its intended temporal context ---
making it inspectable as a first-class \texttt{token@time} object. A model may
be temporally predictive without being temporally traceable: the representation
geometry may not reflect the token's trajectory even when the model output does.
We therefore ask: can a Transformer-style encoder expose a temporally traceable
representation in which the same lexical item, evaluated at different epochs,
has measurably different and semantically appropriate neighborhoods?

We address this question with Timeformer, a family of Transformer-style models
designed around an explicit temporal interface. The family is organized as an
ablation chain: a Static Transformer without time information; an Additive
Time-Conditioned Transformer that injects a global epoch vector; a Token-Time
Transformer that makes lexical identity interact explicitly with continuous
time encodings; and a Memory-Augmented Timeformer that adds causal historical
prototype memory. This design lets us isolate which forms of temporal capacity
support traceable semantic representations, rather than treating temporal
conditioning as a single monolithic architectural choice.

The paper contributes to the literature and state of practice in four ways.
First, it formulates temporal semantic traceability as a \texttt{token@time}
representation problem. Second, it introduces a controlled Timeformer ablation
family for comparing global time conditioning, token-time interaction, and
historical prototype memory. Third, it evaluates temporal traceability with
diagnostics that inspect subject representations directly, including context
drift score, contrastive temporal inversion, and probe-based evaluation. Fourth,
it shows that token-time interaction is the main positive mechanism in the
current setup, while naive mean-pooled historical memory is better understood as
a diagnostic extension than as a robust improvement.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section~2 reviews related
work on diachronic representations, LSCD, temporal conditioning, and diagnostic
evaluation. Section~3 summarizes the controlled benchmark used as the testbed.
Section~4 presents the Timeformer architecture and its ablation variants.
Section~5 describes the evaluation protocol. Section~6 reports the empirical
results, and Section~7 discusses the implications and limitations of the
memory-augmented variant. Section~8 concludes the paper.

\section{Related Work}
% Target length: 1.25--1.5 pages.
Computational approaches to semantic change are commonly framed as lexical
semantic change detection: given language data from different periods, the goal
is to determine whether and how the meaning of a target item has changed.
Diachronic word embeddings provided one of the most influential formulations of
this problem by learning distributional representations for different time
slices and comparing them across periods~\cite{hamilton2016diachronic}. This
family of methods made semantic change operational through geometric movement
in embedding space, often after alignment or normalization across independently
trained temporal slices. The central intuition remains important for this
paper: semantic change should be visible as a change in neighborhood structure.
Our focus, however, is not on aligning independently trained spaces. Instead,
we ask whether a single Transformer-style encoder can expose a representation
that is directly queryable as a lexical item at a specific epoch.

Benchmarks for LSCD have helped standardize evaluation by defining target
words, periods, and gold annotations for semantic change. SemEval-2020 Task 1,
for example, established an unsupervised LSCD setting with multilingual data
and evaluation protocols for binary and graded change
detection~\cite{schlechtweg2020semeval}. Surveys of the field also emphasize
the diversity of corpus designs, annotation regimes, and model families used to
study semantic change~\cite{tahmasebi2021survey}. These resources are essential
for evaluating change in natural corpora, but they also inherit the
observational complexity of historical language: changes in frequency, genre,
domain, and corpus composition may interact with lexical meaning. In this
paper, we use a controlled benchmark as a diagnostic testbed rather than as a
replacement for natural LSCD benchmarks. The controlled setting gives access to
known temporal trajectories and evaluation labels, allowing us to test whether
the intended temporal movement is visible in the model representation.

A second relevant line of work concerns temporal conditioning in neural models.
Rather than training separate models for each period, a temporally conditioned
model can receive time as part of its input or architecture, allowing parameter
sharing across epochs while representations or predictions vary with
time~\cite{dhingra2022timeaware,rosin2022timemasking,loureiro2022timelms}.
For the traceability problem, however, the form of conditioning matters. A
global epoch vector may tell the model which period it is processing, but it
does not necessarily force an interaction between a particular token identity
and that epoch. Timeformer is therefore organized as an ablation chain that
separates global time injection from explicit token-time interaction. This
distinction is important because the paper evaluates not only whether time
improves a prediction, but whether the same token occupies different and
interpretable neighborhoods at different epochs.

Finally, this paper is related to diagnostic evaluation and multi-sense
representations. Probing methods are often used to ask what information is
recoverable from learned representations, but a high probe score does not by
itself explain how individual lexical items move through representation space.
We therefore combine probe-based evaluation with geometric diagnostics, such as
nearest-neighbor context drift, that inspect the representation of the planted
subject token directly. Multi-sense representations are also relevant because
semantic change may involve the coexistence, competition, or bifurcation of
senses rather than a simple shift from one meaning to
another~\cite{giulianelli2020analysing}. Contextualized models such as ELMo and
BERT can assign different representations to the same surface form depending on
context, and this property has been used to track sense-level change across
periods. The memory-augmented variant studied here uses mean-pooled historical
prototypes and is deliberately limited: it tests whether a simple causal memory
helps traceability, while also exposing why more sense-aware memory mechanisms
are needed in future work.

\section{Controlled Benchmark Summary}
% Target length: 1.0 page.
We evaluate Timeformer on a controlled synthetic benchmark designed to make
temporal semantic movement observable. The benchmark is not intended to replace
natural LSCD datasets. Instead, it provides a diagnostic environment in which
the temporal trajectory of each target item is known, the source of semantic
evidence is explicit, and representation-level behavior can be compared against
planted ground truth. Full benchmark construction is treated separately in the
companion benchmark paper; here we summarize only the elements needed to
understand the Timeformer evaluation.

The corpus uses a strict subject--verb--object (SVO) grammar. This structure
isolates subjects as the lexical items with planted temporal trajectories, while
verbs and objects serve as noisy contextual evidence. A subject's meaning at a
given epoch is operationalized as the probability that its sentence is generated
from one of two latent semantic contexts: \(P(A \mid s, t)\) denotes the
context-A probability for subject \(s\) at epoch \(t\). A subject undergoes
semantic change when \(P(A \mid s, t)\) shifts across epochs. The two contexts
are defined by disjoint sets of verbs and objects. The vocabulary contains 30
subjects, 8 verbs, and 8 objects; context A contains verbs
\(\{V1,\ldots,V4\}\) and objects \(\{O1,\ldots,O4\}\), while context B contains
verbs \(\{V5,\ldots,V8\}\) and objects \(\{O5,\ldots,O8\}\).

Subjects are divided into three planted trajectory classes. Stable subjects
maintain an approximately constant \(P(A \mid s, t)\) across all epochs. Drift
subjects follow a monotonic trajectory from near-exclusive context A
(\(P(A \mid s, t) \approx 1\) at \(t0\)) toward near-exclusive context B
(\(P(A \mid s, t) \approx 0\) at \(t9\)). Bifurcating subjects begin in a
dominant context A state and move toward a mixed regime
(\(P(A \mid s, t) \approx 0.5\)) in which both contexts coexist with roughly
equal weight. Full trajectory specifications are given in the companion
benchmark paper; here, these classes define the expected direction and magnitude
of neighborhood movement used in evaluation. The benchmark contains 10 epochs,
\(t0\) through \(t9\), and 10 subjects per trajectory class. This design lets
us test whether a model distinguishes stability, directional drift, and movement
toward a mixed semantic state.

The observed verb and object are informative but deliberately noisy. For the
main corpus, if the planted \texttt{true\_context} is A, each local marker is
sampled from context A with probability \(p_{canon}=0.75\) and from context B
with probability \(0.25\); the reverse holds when the planted context is B.
Thus, local evidence usually points in the intended direction but cannot be
used as a deterministic oracle. The \texttt{true\_context} label is defined by
the planted subject--epoch trajectory, not by the observed verb or object, and
is used only for evaluation.

The benchmark contains several evaluation conditions. The standard test split
uses the same noisy marker process as training. Harder splits include cases in
which the verb contradicts the planted context and cases in which both local
markers contradict it. An additional ambiguous split is generated with
\(p_{canon}=0.50\), making verb and object markers locally uninformative; in
this setting, the subject identity and epoch are the relevant evidence. We also use contrastive evaluations, in which the same subject is presented
under alternative epoch labels to test whether the representation inverts
accordingly, and continuation evaluations, in which the model is tested on
epochs held out from the main training distribution. Both are described in
detail in Section~5.

For this paper, the benchmark plays two roles. First, it gives us direct access
to target trajectories, so we can ask whether the representation of a subject
at \(t0\) and \(t9\) occupies the intended semantic neighborhoods. Second, it
separates context-local cues from temporal cues, allowing the evaluation to
distinguish models that merely exploit observed verbs and objects from models
that encode the interaction between lexical identity and epoch. These
properties make the benchmark suitable for testing temporal traceability, while
also delimiting the scope of the claims: the results are diagnostic evidence in
a controlled setting, not a claim of performance on open-domain historical
language.

\section{Timeformer Architecture}
% Target length: 2.0 pages.
Timeformer is defined as an ablation family rather than as a single model. All
variants share the same basic training interface: an input sentence is encoded
as \texttt{[CLS] S V O [SEP]}, a Transformer encoder produces contextual hidden
states, and an MLM head predicts masked tokens. The representation used for
traceability diagnostics is \(h_s\), the final hidden state at the subject
position. The variants differ only in how epoch information is made available
to the encoder and whether the current subject representation can consult
causal historical prototypes. Table~\ref{tab:model-variants} summarizes the
four variants.

\subsection{Static Transformer}
The Static Transformer is the baseline encoder. It receives token and position
embeddings but no explicit epoch information:

\[
  x_i = e(w_i) + p_i .
\]

The resulting sequence is passed through a small pre-layer-normalized
Transformer encoder. This model can learn corpus-level co-occurrence patterns,
but it has no direct way to distinguish the representation of a subject at
\(t0\) from the same subject at \(t9\), except through the local words observed
in the sentence. It therefore represents the null hypothesis that temporal
behavior can be recovered from static distributional evidence alone.

\subsection{Continuous Time Encoding}
The temporally conditioned variants use a continuous encoding of epoch index
rather than a learned lookup table. For an epoch \(t \in \{0,\ldots,T\}\), the
index is normalized to \([0,1]\), mapped to fixed sinusoidal features, and then
projected by a small MLP:

\[
  \tau(t) = \mathrm{MLP}(\mathrm{sinusoidal}(t/T)).
\]

This choice is meant to support interpolation and extrapolation across the
small number of epochs in the benchmark. A learned embedding table could
memorize the training epochs as unrelated identifiers; a continuous encoding
instead treats epochs as ordered points on a shared temporal scale. The
sinusoidal features here encode epoch index as a scalar on a continuous
temporal scale; they are distinct from the token-position encodings used by
the Transformer encoder itself, which remain standard positional embeddings.

\subsection{Additive Time-Conditioned Transformer}
The Additive Time-Conditioned Transformer injects the same epoch vector into
every token position:

\[
  x_i = e(w_i) + p_i + \tau(t).
\]

This model tests the minimal effect of exposing the encoder to the current
epoch. Because the same temporal offset is added to every token, the model is
given global period information but no token-specific temporal modulation at
the embedding layer. It therefore isolates the question of whether simply
telling the model the epoch is sufficient for traceability.

\subsection{Token-Time Transformer}
The Token-Time Transformer replaces additive conditioning with an explicit
interaction between lexical identity and epoch. For each token, the token
embedding and time encoding are concatenated and projected back to the model
dimension:

\[
  x_i = W [e(w_i); \tau(t)] + p_i .
\]

Here \(e(w_i), \tau(t) \in \mathbb{R}^d\), so the concatenation
\([e(w_i); \tau(t)] \in \mathbb{R}^{2d}\) and \(W \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times 2d}\)
projects back to the model dimension. This is the central Timeformer mechanism
in the paper. Unlike the additive variant, it allows the same epoch vector to
affect different lexical items in different ways before self-attention is applied.
Thus, a subject token can receive a different temporally conditioned
representation from a verb or object
in the same sentence, and the same subject can be represented differently across
epochs. The empirical question is whether this token-time interaction produces
more traceable semantic neighborhoods than global time conditioning alone.

\subsection{Memory-Augmented Timeformer}
The Memory-Augmented Timeformer extends the Token-Time Transformer with a
causal prototype memory. For each subject \(s\) and epoch \(t\), a prototype
\(m(s,t)\) is computed as the mean of subject representations observed in
training sentences for that subject and epoch. Prototypes are computed with
stop-gradient, from training data only, and are served causally: at epoch
\(t_k\), the model may attend only to \(\{m(s,t):t<t_k\}\).

The memory module is a temporal cross-attention layer applied only to the
subject embedding. The current subject embedding acts as the query, and the
historical prototypes for the same subject act as keys and values. The updated
subject embedding is then inserted back into the token sequence before the
Transformer encoder:

\[
  \tilde{x}_s =
  x_s + g \cdot
  \left(\mathrm{LN}(x_s + \mathrm{Attn}(x_s, M_s, M_s)) - x_s\right),
\]

where \(M_s\) is the causal history for subject \(s\), and \(g \in [0,1]\) is
a learned scalar gate initialized to zero so that at the start of training
\(\tilde{x}_s = x_s\) and the memory path is inactive. The formula
interpolates between the original embedding (\(g=0\)) and the
layer-normalized attended representation (\(g=1\)), allowing the model to
gradually open the memory channel as training progresses. Applying the memory
update before the textual encoder lets the subsequent self-attention layers
propagate historical information from the subject to the other token positions,
creating a path by which the MLM objective can reward useful memory use.

In this paper, the memory-augmented variant is treated as a diagnostic
extension. It tests whether mean-pooled causal history helps temporal
traceability beyond token-time interaction. Because a single mean prototype can
collapse coexisting senses within an epoch, the model is not expected to solve
sense bifurcation by construction; this limitation is part of the motivation
for sense-aware memory in future work.

\section{Evaluation Protocol}
% Target length: 1.25--1.5 pages.
All model variants are trained with the same self-supervised objective: masked
language modeling over the synthetic SVO corpus. The planted
\texttt{true\_context} label is never used to train the encoder. It is used
only after training, to evaluate whether the learned representation contains
the temporal semantic information implied by the benchmark trajectories. This
separation is important because the intended use case is representation
learning from corpora in which semantic-change labels are not available.

The main object of evaluation is \(h_s\), the final hidden state at the subject
position. This choice follows directly from the benchmark design: subjects are
the lexical items with planted temporal trajectories, while verbs and objects
serve as noisy contextual evidence. We therefore evaluate whether \(h_s\)
encodes the intended context of the subject at a given epoch. Sentence-level
representations based on \texttt{[CLS]} are also computed, but the subject
representation is the primary diagnostic target.

We use four complementary diagnostics. First, a linear probe is trained on
\(h_s\) from the standard test split to predict \texttt{true\_context} and is
then evaluated on each split. This measures whether context information is
linearly recoverable from the representation, but it does not by itself
establish traceability. Second, we compute a context drift score over nearest
neighbors. For each epoch and subject class, we retrieve the \(k\) nearest
neighbors of each subject representation% TODO: state k value explicitly
\ and measure the proportion assigned to context A, with \(k\) fixed at the
same value across all models and splits. A traceable representation should show
a strong movement from A-like to B-like neighborhoods for drifting subjects,
weaker movement for bifurcating subjects, and comparatively limited movement
for stable subjects. Third, we use contrastive temporal examples in which the
same subject token is presented with two different epoch labels. We define the
contrastive sign-flip rate as the proportion of such pairs for which swapping
the epoch label reverses the nearest-neighbor context assignment --- from
A-dominant to B-dominant or vice versa. A model without temporal capacity is
expected to produce a sign-flip rate of zero, since the same token yields the
same representation regardless of the epoch label provided. Fourth, we evaluate
continuation examples from held-out later epochs% TODO: specify which epochs are held out (e.g. t8--t9)
\ to test whether temporal behavior extends beyond the training distribution.

The ablation comparisons follow the architecture chain. The gain from
time-conditioning is measured by comparing the Additive Time-Conditioned
Transformer to the Static Transformer, primarily on the ambiguous split where
local markers are uninformative. The gain from token-time interaction is
measured by comparing the Token-Time Transformer to the additive variant on the
same split. The memory contribution is measured by comparing the
Memory-Augmented Timeformer to the Token-Time Transformer on continuation and
contrastive diagnostics.

For the memory-augmented model, we include controls that test whether any gain
comes from the correct subject history rather than from extra parameters. In
the shuffled-memory control, a subject receives prototypes from a randomly
selected different subject, establishing whether subject-specific identity in
the causal history matters or whether any historical context produces the same
effect. In the no-history control, the memory path is present but receives no
valid historical prototypes. We also report an oracle-memory diagnostic, where
available, to test whether perfect historical prototypes would be sufficient to
improve the same metrics. These controls are interpreted conservatively: memory
is considered useful only when it improves over the Token-Time Transformer and
over inappropriate or empty histories.

\section{Results}
% Target length: 2.5--3.0 pages.
We report results over three training runs. Unless otherwise noted, values are
means over seeds, with the standard deviation shown in parentheses when space
allows. The main finding is that token-time interaction produces a much
clearer temporal movement in representation space than the Static Transformer.
The memory-augmented variant is informative as a diagnostic extension, but it
does not provide a robust improvement over the Token-Time Transformer.

\subsection{Temporal Neighborhood Movement}
% Main claim: B2b produces a stronger temporal neighborhood transition than B1.
% Use class-specific curves to avoid claiming stable subjects are perfectly flat.
Figure~\ref{fig:context-drift} shows the context drift score across epochs. For
drifting subjects, the Static Transformer moves only weakly from an A-like
neighborhood to a B-like neighborhood: the estimated context-A proportion among
nearest neighbors drops from \(0.618\) at \(t0\) to \(0.387\) at \(t9\), a
change of \(-0.231\). The Token-Time Transformer shows a much stronger
transition, from \(0.847\) to \(0.233\), a change of \(-0.614\). This is the
clearest evidence for temporal traceability in the current experiments: the
same subject token occupies substantially different neighborhoods when queried
at different epochs.

The class-specific curves are also useful as controls. Stable subjects are not
perfectly flat, especially for temporally conditioned models, but their
movement is much smaller and less aligned with a full A-to-B transition. Across
seeds, the stable-class change is \(+0.020\) for the Static Transformer,
\(-0.203\) for the Token-Time Transformer, and \(-0.153\) for the
Memory-Augmented Timeformer. Bifurcating subjects show intermediate behavior:
the Token-Time Transformer moves from \(0.895\) to \(0.521\), consistent with a
trajectory toward a mixed state rather than a complete transition to context B.
Thus, the geometric diagnostic distinguishes the qualitative trajectory
classes better than a single aggregate probe score.

\subsection{Probe and Contrastive Diagnostics}
The linear probe results are supportive but less decisive than the neighborhood
analysis. On the ambiguous split, where local verb and object markers are
uninformative, the Static Transformer reaches \(0.577\) accuracy, the Additive
Time-Conditioned Transformer reaches \(0.573\), and the Token-Time Transformer
reaches \(0.599\). The corresponding mean delta for token-time interaction is
\(+0.026\), but it is seed-sensitive: the three runs give \(+0.053\),
\(+0.005\), and \(+0.021\). This confirms that probe accuracy alone should not
carry the central claim. It measures recoverability of the planted label, not
the geometry of temporal movement.

The contrastive sign-flip diagnostic gives a different view. The Static
Transformer has a sign-flip rate of \(0.000\), as expected for a model without
explicit time. The Additive Time-Conditioned Transformer averages \(0.690\),
while the Token-Time Transformer averages \(0.517\). This ordering may appear
counterintuitive given that the Token-Time Transformer produces a much stronger
neighborhood drift. The decoupling arises because sign-flip rate and drift score
measure distinct properties: sign-flip counts the proportion of contrastive
pairs in which the nearest-neighbor context assignment crosses the A/B midpoint,
while drift score measures the full magnitude of trajectory movement across
epochs. A global additive epoch offset displaces all token representations
uniformly, which can push many assignments across the decision boundary without
producing coherent subject-level trajectories. Token-time interaction modulates
each token according to its identity, producing more structured and
subject-specific movement --- evidenced by the stronger drift --- but not
necessarily crossing the contrastive threshold in more pairs. We therefore treat
contrastive sign flip as evidence that time conditioning changes model behavior
at all, and the neighborhood trajectories of Figure~\ref{fig:context-drift} as
the more diagnostic measure of temporal traceability.

\section{Discussion}
% Target length: 1.25--1.5 pages.
The results support a clear conclusion: temporal traceability is a geometric
property of the representation that does not reduce to predictive accuracy. The
Token-Time Transformer improves probe accuracy on the ambiguous split by only
\(+0.026\) on average --- a modest and seed-sensitive gain. Yet the same model
shows a neighborhood drift of \(-0.614\) for drifting subjects, compared to
\(-0.231\) for the Static Transformer --- a difference that is stable across
seeds and visible in the class-specific trajectory curves of
Figure~\ref{fig:context-drift}. This dissociation is the central empirical
finding: a model can condition its outputs on epoch information without exposing
a representation in which the same lexical item occupies meaningfully different
neighborhoods at different epochs. The converse also holds: a model with
explicit token-time interaction can produce coherent geometric movement even
when the improvement in probe accuracy is modest. Evaluating traceability
therefore requires diagnostics that inspect the geometry of the representation
directly, not only those that measure label recoverability.

A linear probe trained on \(h_s\) establishes whether the planted context label
is linearly recoverable from the subject representation. A high probe score is
consistent with traceability, but it does not establish it: the probe could be
recovering a global epoch signal distributed across the representation rather
than a subject-specific temporal trajectory. The ambiguous split is the most
informative probe condition precisely because local verb and object markers are
uninformative there, forcing any gain to come from the interaction between
subject identity and epoch. Even in this condition, differences across model
variants are small and unstable across seeds. This does not mean the probe is
uninformative --- it provides a lower bound on temporal information encoded in
the representation --- but it is not the appropriate primary evidence for a
traceability claim. The nearest-neighbor diagnostics, which directly inspect
where each subject representation sits relative to its temporal context, are
more suitable for that purpose.

The additive variant demonstrates that exposing the encoder to the epoch is not
sufficient for traceability. A global additive epoch offset shifts all token
representations by the same vector, leaving the relative geometry of subject
representations at different epochs only weakly constrained. The model knows
which period it is processing, but this knowledge does not translate into
subject-specific trajectory structure --- as confirmed by the high sign-flip
rate without a corresponding coherent drift. Token-time interaction changes this
fundamentally: by concatenating each token embedding with the epoch encoding
before projection, the model allows the same epoch vector to affect different
tokens differently. The same epoch produces a distinct transformation for the
subject token and for the verb or object in the same sentence, and the same
subject receives a different embedding at different epochs. This is the
mechanism behind the coherent neighborhood trajectories in the drift analysis:
the representation of each subject is a function of both its identity and its
epoch, not merely of the local context words observed in the sentence.

The Memory-Augmented Timeformer tests the simplest form of causal memory: a
mean of historical subject representations per epoch. The oracle diagnostic
clarifies the failure mode. Perfect historical prototypes improve the
contrastive sign-flip rate but not continuation accuracy, and learned prototypes
produce near-zero average gain over the Token-Time Transformer. The limitation
is structural: a single mean prototype per subject per epoch collapses the
internal distribution of that subject's representations at that time. For
drifting subjects, where all representations in an epoch are drawn from the same
dominant context, the mean captures the trajectory reasonably well. For
bifurcating subjects, which develop two coexisting senses in later epochs, the
mean is a centroid between two clusters --- a point that represents neither
sense well. This is not a failure of the memory architecture, but a failure of
the aggregation function. Useful causal memory for traceable representations
should preserve the internal structure of historical representations --- for
example, through mixture models, exemplar-based attention, or clustering over
historical states. This limitation is the direct motivation for the sense-aware
memory mechanism studied in the companion paper.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper studied whether a Transformer-style encoder can expose a temporally
traceable representation --- one in which the same lexical item, queried at
different epochs, occupies neighborhoods that reflect its intended temporal
context. The answer, in the controlled benchmark used here, is conditional:
explicit token-time interaction is necessary. A static encoder and a globally
time-conditioned encoder both fail to produce coherent subject-level
trajectories. An encoder that conditions each token embedding on the epoch
before self-attention is applied does produce such trajectories, with a
neighborhood drift for drifting subjects that is more than twice that of the
static baseline (\(-0.614\) vs. \(-0.231\)).

The contribution is narrow and deliberate. Token-time interaction supports
temporal traceability in a controlled diagnostic setting; it is not a claim
about open-domain historical language or about the superiority of any
particular architecture. The evaluation protocol --- combining nearest-neighbor
drift, contrastive sign-flip rate, probe-based accuracy, and continuation
diagnostics --- also contributes a method for separating representational
traceability from predictive performance, a distinction the results show to be
empirically real.

The memory-augmented variant clarifies what remains unsolved. Mean-pooled
historical prototypes do not produce a robust improvement over token-time
interaction, and the oracle diagnostic identifies the reason: single-prototype
aggregation collapses coexisting senses that bifurcating subjects develop in
later epochs. Sense-aware memory mechanisms --- capable of preserving
distributional structure rather than averaging it away --- are the natural next
step and are the subject of the companion paper.
